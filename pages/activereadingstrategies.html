What is Active Reading?

Active reading means thinking while you read. You choose strategies based on the text type (poetry, fiction, nonfiction) and your purpose (understand an author, answer questions, prepare for an essay, or read for pleasure).

Effective Strategies You Can Use Choose what works
  • Pre-reading questions: What’s the topic? What does the title suggest? What do I already know? What do I wonder?
  • Define unfamiliar terms: highlight words you don’t know and look them up.
  • Find the main idea/thesis: put it in brackets and add a star.
  • Margin notes: summarize, question, agree/disagree, note keywords.
  • Turn headings into questions: read to answer your own questions.
  • Visual organizers: outlines, flow charts, diagrams.
  • “What it says / what it does”: main idea + purpose of each paragraph.
  • One-page summary: main ideas + 1–2 key examples.
  • Create an exam question: challenge understanding.
  • Teach it: explaining out loud strengthens long-term memory.
Deep Reading Mindset Think beyond facts

Deep reading isn’t just memorizing names/dates. It’s connecting details, using background knowledge, asking “why/how,” and noticing what the text suggests—even when it’s not stated directly.

SQ3R Strategy

SQ3R helps you “get it” the first time you read: Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review.

How SQ3R Works 5 steps
  • Survey (Skim): preview headings, bold words, charts—get the big picture.
  • Question: create questions from what you preview.
  • Read: read to answer your questions (stay focused).
  • Recite: say answers out loud; jot notes for later study.
  • Review: revisit the text; answer leftover questions and retell key ideas.
Quick win: If you only do one part, do Question. Questions turn reading into a mission.

Active Reading for Short Stories

Fiction invites you into a world created by the author. Reading stories is an active process: you build a “movie” in your mind, adjust your understanding as events unfold, and think about meaning.

Core Strategies for Stories Use during reading
  • QUESTION: Why do characters act this way? What causes events? Why include certain details?
  • VISUALIZE: picture scenes; revise your mental image; use visualization to clear confusion.
  • PREDICT: what might happen next? What hints suggest an outcome?
  • CONNECT: link events to your life, the real world, and other moments in the story.
  • RESPOND: what does the story mean? How does it make you feel? What does it teach about life?

Mini Prompts (Quick Writing)

  • I predict… because the title/details suggest…
  • I visualize… (describe the scene like a movie).
  • I connect… (to my life / real world / another event).
  • I wonder… (a question that pushes deeper meaning).
  • This matters because… (theme / lesson / message).

Assignment: Active Reading — “Games at Twilight”

Task: Answer the questions below using complete, descriptive sentences. Each question is labeled with the active-reading strategy. Make reference to the question in your answer.

Questions (9)
  1. PREDICT: What does the title of the story suggest about its content?
  2. VISUALIZE / CONNECT: Put yourself in Ravi’s position. What would you have done after Raghu left the shed area?
  3. PREDICT / CONNECT: If you could speak to Ravi, what would you tell him about games?
  4. QUESTION: In the story, where does Ravi hide?
  5. QUESTION: Even though he wasn’t caught, what causes Ravi to lose the game?
  6. QUESTION: How does Ravi feel about Raghu?
  7. VISUALIZE: Based on Ravi’s choices, what do we know about his personality?
  8. RESPOND: At the end of the story, what bitter lesson does Ravi learn?
  9. RESPOND: Will Ravi’s “sense of insignificance” remain strong? Explain.

Assessment Criteria

Learning Target (6/6): Exemplary comprehension and clear accomplishment of the objective. Demonstrates exemplary active reading skills (predict, visualize, connect, question, respond). All activities complete and correct. Ideas are thoughtful and unique.

Written Expression (6/6): Varied sentence structure and vocabulary. Carefully proofread (few/no errors). Insightful connections, logical organization, easy to read.

Time plan (45–60 min): 10 min preview + question • 20–25 min read + annotate • 15–20 min answer questions • 5 min review + proofread
